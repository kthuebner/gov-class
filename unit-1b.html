Section One

Types of Government

Every government can be categorized by who holds power and how that authority is justified. The key question is not just who rules, but what claim they make to make that rule legitimate.

Democracy / Republic
Rule by the People
Claim to Legitimacy: Popular Sovereignty

Government derives its authority from the people. Citizens elect leaders who govern on their behalf. Widely considered the only truly legitimate form of government.

Examples: United States, most of Europe, India, Japan.

Monarchy
Rule by a King or Queen
Claim to Legitimacy: Divine Right

Power is held by a single hereditary ruler. Historically, monarchs claimed authority from God — to question the king was to question God.

Examples: Medieval European kingdoms. Modern constitutional monarchies (UK, Sweden) retain a monarch but with little real power.

Oligarchy
Rule by a Small Group
Claim to Legitimacy: Wealth or Military Force

Power is concentrated among a small elite. Legitimacy is difficult to establish and often depends on coercion. Many authoritarian states function as oligarchies in practice.

Examples: Ancient Sparta; Russia today, where a small circle of oligarchs and security officials holds real power.

Theocracy
Rule by Religious Leaders
Claim to Legitimacy: Divine Authority

Government is led by religious authorities who claim to enforce God's law. Questioning the government means questioning God — dissent is treated as heresy.

Examples: Medieval papal states; Iran today, where religious scholars hold supreme authority above elected officials.

Dictatorship
Rule by a Single Person
Claim to Legitimacy: Military Force

A single leader holds power, often having seized it by force. Dictators typically use the military and coercion to maintain control. "Might makes right."

Examples: Adolf Hitler in Nazi Germany; Kim Jong-un in North Korea; Muammar Gaddafi in Libya.

Hybrid Regime
Democracy as a Facade
Claim to Legitimacy: Rigged Elections

Holds elections and maintains the formal structures of democracy — but systematically rigs the game so the ruling party cannot lose. Courts, press, and genuine opposition are gradually dismantled. Scholars call this democratic backsliding.

Examples: Hungary under Orban; Turkey under Erdogan; Venezuela under Maduro; Russia.

Key Principles of American Democracy

American democracy rests on eight foundational principles built into the Constitution and tested throughout American history. Click each card to flip it and explore the definition and an example.

1
Popular Sovereignty
click to flip
Definition
All government authority comes from the people. The government rules only with the consent of the governed.
Citizens vote for leaders, sign petitions, and can amend the Constitution. Power flows upward from the governed.
2
Limited Government
click to flip
Definition
Government power is defined and bounded. It cannot act outside those limits.
The Constitution lists what Congress can do. The Bill of Rights lists what government cannot do — restrict speech, conduct unreasonable searches, deny due process.
3
Separation of Powers
click to flip
Definition
Government power is split among three branches. No single branch can do everything.
Congress passes laws. The President enforces them. The Supreme Court interprets them. Each branch operates in its own lane.
4
Checks & Balances
click to flip
Definition
Each branch can limit the others, preventing any one from abusing power.
Congress can impeach the President. The President nominates Supreme Court justices, but the Senate confirms them. The Court can strike down laws as unconstitutional.
5
Rule of Law
click to flip
Definition
No one is above the law — not citizens, not officials, not the President. Rules apply equally to everyone.
Even the President can face criminal charges. Courts operate independently to ensure the law is applied fairly regardless of who is involved.
6
Majority Rule
click to flip
Definition
The side that wins elections earns the right to govern. Losers must accept the legitimate outcome.
The winning party takes power. The losing side accepts the result and competes in the next election rather than rejecting the results of the last one.
7
Minority Rights
click to flip
Definition
No majority has the right to strip away the basic rights of a minority. Majority rule has limits.
The Bill of Rights protects individual freedoms regardless of popular opinion. Courts can strike down laws that violate minority rights even if passed by large majorities.
8
Federalism
click to flip
Definition
Power is divided between the national government and the states. Neither can simply absorb the other's authority.
Education and law enforcement are largely left to the states. National defense, immigration, and currency are federal responsibilities.
